Yield! Fall of Rome

Unity · C# · 4X Strategy
View on Steam
Topics
My Role

System & Content Designer, Gameplay Engineer. Joined as the third team member and shaped core systems from prototype through Steam release over 2.5 years.

01

Building an empire

Design
"Decisions are made once" We aligned on this principle early on as one of our main tools to keep game duration low compared to the 8 hour sessions our audience may know from the Civilization franchise. Yet we questioned it many times, because no undoing of mistakes means that to feel succesful, players need to have a working mental model of their options. This steepened Yield's learning curve and put constraints on content design to minimize the frustration around making permanent choices with limited information.
"Instant Rewards" An incredibly satisfying human experience is becoming demonstrably better at something. Yield's instant rewards allow players to instantly realize when they have made a mistake (like not having enough gold left over to buy a unit) or when they have done well (like getting just enough population to grow your city). Instant rewards also make it easier to celebrate player actions: when a player builds a farm and their city grows as a result, Yield can match the importance of that event right then and there with celebratory feedback, whereas a game like Civ VI with yields per turn, is forced to disconnect the moment when a player invests in their growth from the moment that decision pays off.
Leaving your mark on the map When I joined the team, improvements always rewarded as much production as population and skill in the economic snowball was expressed mostly by analyzing which improvement type you should unlock to reward you the most total yields given the land you could settle. I saw a huge opportunity to make improving your land in different ways also feel different and over time tied a lot of systems into this - realizing archetypes such as "The Wonder Builder", that players could lean into by focusing on improvements that reward production and policies that give rewards for building wonders in addition to a more defensive playstyle since expansion necessarily becomes slow when your economic engine revolves around buildings that cost a lot of resources. The result of these archetypes are empires that look visually distinct on the map.
Replayability To keep players engaged, Yield heavily relies on players wanting to jump right into the next game to do things better and for stories worth telling to emerge organically in this process. To accomplish this, we invite new players to play Yield's campaign mode, which lets players live through historic events in a series of single missions, during each of which player's are able to make progress along many axes: leveling their king, making allies, unlocking all their milestones or unique units like the legion - each punctuated with highly evocative effects. It's near impossible to unlock everything in a single campaign and the combinatorics of all possible unlocks uniquely shape players capabilities to face the campaign's challenges.
02

Combat

Design
Coloring the mapExpanding your borders and engulfing other empires in your own is an experience many strategy game players describe as deeply satisfying. To allow for a meaningful back and forth within Yield's tight time budget, combat needed to start early, be focused and have big swings. We designed for this with powerful active abilities your king can unlock, unlockable units with unique gameplay niches and combat rules that are reminiscent of how chess pieces take out one another.
Unit archetypes One of our goals was to never let a unit that a player has built become useless over the course of the game. Other games achieve this by letting warriors upgrade into swordsmen while preserving all of the unit's modifiers, but we felt that the scope of Yield lent itself towards a different approach: making each unit type situationally useful. We felt like this approach succeeded when during a playtest a player built a horseman despite having the much more technologically advanced knights unlocked - both are great against units with low defense: knights can keep attacking when they score a kill and horsemen can attack and retreat out of danger immediately after. These 2 unit types are surprisingly useful together - horsemen would go in first and weaken the enemy before retreating and knights would finish them off. Synergies between unit types inspired us to redesign our entire tech tree while keeping economic synergies balanced at the same time.
Active abilitiesDesigning these felt like bringing a moba into the 4X world and players loved them during playtests. While they delivered on the power fantasy of your king being an inspiring leader to your troops, balancing them for multiplayer was another beast. We found that requiring deep investment in your kings level to unlock the powerful active combat abilities helped both with balance and with building further endearment in your king during campaigns.
03

Achieving Victory

Design
Finding meaningBefore the current modular Crown system in which players can design themselves which actions should reward how many Crowns and how many of them are required for a victory, Yield used to be about achieving the highest score within a turn limit, with each action from building an improvement to capturing a city contributing to this score. While being easy to explain, this resulted in a grind for victory points, that ultimately made especially the last turns of each match feel slow and full of tedious tasks, while matches would also end at an arbitrary turn limit sometimes without any meaningful action and sometimes right in the middle of it. In looking for a new system we set our eyes on what actions in Yield felt dramatic enough to be game deciding and allowing for many possible narrative arcs for games, all centered around "achieving dominance" on the map.
Technical OwnershipThis was the first major system that I owned the implementation of. It required a rewrite across how matches were created, new UI for allowing modification by players, how the game checked whether victory conditions were met, how we created the space to display modifiable victory conditions when they also need to be able to be displayed in different languages and how our AI pursued victory conditions or prevented the player from achieving them. It also brought up many questions of how transparent the AI's progress towards their own victory conditions should be for the player, as losing to events happening in undiscovered land were called out as frustrating by many.
04

Iteration Process

Process
Consistent playtesting Throughout development we kept reminding each other to never be embarrassed to show off our progress. This expressed itself through us joining every festival out there and gathering feedback live at Gamescom & PAX. At Gamescom in particular, we felt so moved by feedback, that we came back every day with a new build to test. Between playtests online that I led, we gained insights that drove +30% longer play duration and +20% higher replay intent.
The game as our only artifactWhile I pioneered documentation standards to be used by our remote team, which later were also adopted by our publisher for release management, our focus in all our design conversations was on our prototype. This helped ground us in the principles we had established early on, which defined our niche in the market as we saw it, and made us iterate quickly. During this process we threw out many ideas like much more complex adjacency rules, armor as a unit property, a secondary currency that could be spent as tokens on special unlocks - similar to the current policies, a specialist system of farmers, craftsmen, merchants and nobles to modify improvements and many more. Had we not focused on our prototype as our only artifact, I think it likely that we would have made a much more complex game with much longer play sessions, making Yield less distinct from genre staples like Civilization or Old World.
05

Engineering Tools

Technical
TestingExpanded our existing test kit to allow for AIs to play against each other in fully configurable scenarios, catching 73 bugs through thousands of simulated scenarios in the last 2 weeks before release that we would not have caught otherwise.
Internal Match EditorBuilt an in-editor mode for devs to manually design maps, complete with AI starting locations, starting units, starting policies and milestone progress and many more settings. It also can be opened mid match in the editor to modify everything from city population to unit skills to spawning in units on the map for each faction. This tool easily cut the time I spent chasing down bugs and testing new abilites down to a third of what it used to be and coworkers gave similarly enthusiastic feedback throughout my development of this tool.

Design contributions

Design
🎬
Topic GIF
Mission Statements These have changed over time under the pressure of a community yearning for new content - originally they had much stricter language around sticking to firaxis' vision, which was partially inspired by a wish for affiliation with firaxis by the owners of the mod at the time. But thanks to many challenging discussions, the mod team is now fully aligned with the wishes of the players using the mod.
  • Provide a leveled playing field in multiplayer regardless of your Civilization/Leader selection
  • Adjust unfair game mechanics or bugs while keeping the original design
  • Expand original mechanics to provide more strategic diversity
  • Improve upon the approachabilty for new players compared to the base game
A case study: ChinaFiraxis blessed the chinese community with the ability to represent their country with 5 different leaders - providing a challenging task for any designer to give them each a unique gameplay niche, given the fact that they all share the chinese civilization ability, a unique unit and a unique improvement. To balance those gameplay niches against one another is another challenge, for which we will need to establish that each civilization has to operate within some fixed power budget, which is already split between mentioned unique units, improvements and the civilization ability, which each can't be too weak, less they feel underwhelming.

Therefore, to make each leader feel maximally unique, we would want to allocate as much of their power budget as possible into their leader ability. Here is where we run into the final challenge: kublai china shares a leader ability with kublai mongolia, which shares their civ ability with genghis khan - maximizing the impact of kublai's leader ability would mean that the mongolian civ ability would have to be made weaker or kublai mongolia would be an incredibly overpowered leader. Yet, the mongolian civ ability has been around since the game's release and has aged like fine wine over the years, commonly being regarded as one of the best designed and most iconic pieces of content.

My solution to this complex chain of dependencies was to propose to break an unspoken rule: by tying the rewards of china's unique improvement to which leader was constructing it, we gave each china a different reason to build the great wall and different gameplay consequences, thus each china could spread their power budget more evenly between civ and leader ability while maintaining a unique gameplay niche, thus aleviating balancing pressure on kublai mongolia and allowing us to keep the iconic mongolian ability.
02

Organization

Process
Managing egosOne of the fun parts of working with fellow volunteers is that the most common reason for participation seems to be rooted in some form of need to be seen as great. People motivated by this have made amazing contributions to the BBG project, but they have also all burned out quick. I believe that great ideas deserve to be celebrated, yet design is about execution and a project of the scale of BBG requires people of all backgrounds to chime in and remix their best ideas; it does not lend itself well to anyone trying to see themselves as the sole hero of design proposals for any extended period of time. Over the years I've seen patterns in how people approach joining our team and I've improved at helping them manage their expectations and communication with the team to succeed.
Decision makersOver the years we've experimented with many forms of finalizing design decisions: from polls to the whole community, to a single decision maker, to a counsel of specialists, to a small team overseeing and polling a larger group of specialists. Ultimately, I have felt that there is no single best structure and that the success metrics to actually pay attention to are how understood the community felt at large and how quickly the mod team could respond to emerging unhealthy metas. Doing well in both these metrics requires the decision makers to often and deeply engage with raised concerns and proposals - which is hard labor and may seem more boring then some elegant, perfect structure of organization that would finally make everyone happy, but it works and finding enjoyment in this is now one of the key qualities we look for in electing new decision makers.
Data collectionData should be a key aspect of any balancing effort, yet it has long been a blindspot of BBG. Part of the reason for this is structural: the number of games payed at a top level is miniscule and casual games often have mixed skill levels, because the player population and the long match duration do not support skill-based matchmaking and therefore deliver inconsistent data. The key factor working in our favor however is time, Civ VI has been around for so long and many civs have not changed across multiple seasons, allowing for some quantitative insighs into winrates and other metrics. Beyond these efforts, we also collect qualitative data from our community via surveys some time after each major content patch. These insights helped guide us in large scale efforts such as rebalancing the base game's victory conditions.

Foreword

While I am educating myself on how to effectively develop indie games, this project is a learning opportunity in many more ways to me. As such, I may spend more time on certain features like using particle systems to simulate wave effects than may be advisable at this stage of the project.

01

Why this project

Process
Hole in the marketBeing a fan of 4X games, I am very familiar with the game Spore, a hugely ambitious project simulating the journey from cellular life to galactic civilizations - it is a game that elicits nostalgia in many, spawning new projects inspired by its art and narrative even today. One of it's main critiques is that it feels like 3 loosely strung together and not fully fleshed out minigames. It's cell stage in particular is promising as a starting point for a new take - it's associated mechanics are reminiscent of recent hits capturing the cultural moment like vampire survivors or agar.io, yet stand out against a broader roguelike market that does not see a lot of skill expression in momentum-driven movement mechanics or melee combat.
Why do so few roguelikes incorporate momentum-driven movement?Inertia, unresponsiveness and clunkyness are often called out as a point of frustration for players, yet these concepts are pivotal to gamefeel in racing games or making a weapon feel heavy in souls-likes. They enforce some amount of planning of movement inputs, which may subtract from the fast paced action of bullet hell games, which would rather reward players for fast reactions. Racing games and souls-likes both are also very careful to maintain the hope that you could still achieve 1st place or defeat the boss, no matter how many times you crashed or got hit. Roguelikes on the other hand, make mistakes very visible in the form of lost health, typically not easily regained and punishing through permadeath. Thus, to make a roguelike that succeeds in offering deeper skill expression in movement, my goals are to set expectations for players so that they expect to need to plan their movement inputs in advance and look forward to doing so well, as well as to design systems that allow players to easily move past their mistakes.

Process
What other games does my audience play?
  • Roguelikes such as Vampire Survivors, Brotato and The Binding of Isaac
  • Deckbuilders and autobattlers like Slay the Spire, Hearthstone, The Bazaar and TFT

What are their motivations?

  • Mastery (learning to use the games mechanics to overcome its challenges and measuring that growth)
Where can I find my audience?On social media, particularly youtube shorts and tik tok, but predominantly through steam's algorithm.
03

Landing a vibe

Design
ArtstyleI have considered going for "cartoonish", "cute", "eldritch horror" or "realistic" and looking at my audience it's clear that they are on the younger side and find themselves playing mostly cartoonish games with some leaning more towards blood and violence while others lean towards cute. I have decided to go with cartoonish, leaning towards cute myself as well, as it is more inline with expectations from spore, less problematic for content creators and has likely a greater overlap with relevant audiences than other "vibes". There is a huge contrast between cute and horror and I am considering using that as "wow factor" for marketing by including niche build orders that devolve into this horror aspect but would have to be deliberately chosen and come with appropriate warnings so that players unwilling to engage with it can avoid it.
NarrativeIn a game about evolution, the questions: "Where from?" and "Where to?" have to be considered and they mirror humanity's own search for meaning. Leaving only vague hints as to your origin but defining a clear goal creates mystery that makes players want to learn more about this world, thus I am currently running with the idea of showing the player character as the product of aliens delivering life to a virgin world; yet you are not the only species that has thus arrived.
04

Designing for momemtum-driven movement

Design
CombatThe setting of evolving cells already implies that combat may get up close and personal and a focus on explicitly colliding with enemies or avoiding collisions will allow for further skill expression than would be possible in a "bullet hell" type game - enemies changing their trajectory or speed is expected while projectiles doing the same may come off as surprising. Additionally there are many ways to make the angle of these collisions matter as different body parts collide. "Bullet hell" type games also tend to struggle with visual clarity as any given enemy may produce any number of projectiles, that need to be recognizable, if the player is supposed to avoid them. A focus on collisions frees up much of my visual real estate for effects that look dramatic.
Health Out of all the lose conditions in video games, health is easily the most commonly understood and I want to use words players already understand as much as possible given my nonexistent budget to explain niche game mechanics through marketing material. Another mechanic players commonly understand from agar.io is their player character growing in size as they eat food or enemies. Furthermore, I want to have high stakes near death moments that don't drag on - players should generally feel very safe to create contrast to when they are in mortal danger. With my goal in mind of allowing players to move past their mistakes I give players enough health to take multiple hits and allow them to regain health by leveling up. Players level up by eating enough food. The food required to advance to the next level gets progressively higher with player level. When players take damage they lose both health and level, thus players that struggle and get hit a lot are always just a few bites of food away from regaining their health. Having a larger character model becomes an easily identifiable sign of doing well and players can always achieve a quick growth spurt from low levels by doing well for a short time or getting lucky, while maintaining that size becomes a skill expression as larger models are also easier to hit.
MovementInertia becomes incredibly frustrating when the time required to change direction in response to an object appearing at the edge of the screen gets smaller than the average human reaction time - I plan to counteract this by zooming out as players go faster and progressively accelerating turn speed by the magnitude of the change in direction the player wishes to make.
